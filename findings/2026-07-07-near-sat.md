# Near-Saturation Experiment — 2026-07-07

Tests whether prefix-aware reordering + dynamic chunking + aging improve tail
latency in the near-saturation regime (~85% GPU utilization), where a transient
waiting queue exists but does not grow unboundedly.

**Model:** Qwen2.5-Coder-7B-Instruct  
**Hardware:** NVIDIA RTX 4090 (24 GB)  
**Workload:** BurstGPT (primary), ShareGPT (secondary)  
**Script:** `scripts/run_near_sat_bench.sh`  
**Conditions:** baseline (REORDER=0, CHUNK=0) · combined (REORDER=1, CHUNK=1) · aging T=2s (REORDER=1, CHUNK=1, AGING_THRESHOLD_MS=2000)

---

## Regime Characterization

Finding the correct near-saturation operating point required a rate sweep.
BurstGPT at 4 req/s turned out to be at or above saturation (throughput ceiling
≈ 4.02 req/s); 3 req/s is the confirmed near-saturation point.
ShareGPT's GPU utilization signal is unreliable — see §ShareGPT below.

| Dataset  | Arrival rate | Throughput  | GPU util | Regime                            |
|----------|-------------|-------------|----------|-----------------------------------|
| BurstGPT | 4 req/s     | 2.73 req/s  | —        | oversaturated (queue unbounded)   |
| BurstGPT | 3 req/s     | 2.37 req/s  | **85%**  | near-saturation ✓                 |
| ShareGPT | 4 req/s     | 3.43 req/s  | 75%      | underloaded — see §ShareGPT       |
| ShareGPT | 5 req/s     | 3.99 req/s  | 68%      | (GPU util unreliable, see §ShareGPT) |

## BurstGPT Results (Primary)

Arrival rate: 3 req/s · 150 prompts · max-seqs=32 · aging T=2 s

Results below are from Run 2 (2026-07-08, with KV hit rate instrumented).
Run 1 (2026-07-07) showed the same directional improvements but different
absolute values (E2EL p95 baseline: 8134 ms vs 15289 ms here), reflecting
high variance inherent to the near-saturation regime with 150 prompts.

| Metric          | Baseline  | Combined    | Δ combined     | Aging (T=2s) | Δ aging        |
|-----------------|-----------|-------------|----------------|--------------|----------------|
| TTFT p50 (ms)   | 91.9      | 86.2        | −5.1%          | 92.1         | +0.2%          |
| TTFT p95 (ms)   | 302.7     | 297.0       | −1.9%          | 299.4        | −1.1%          |
| TPOT p50 (ms)   | 17.9      | 17.6        | −1.9%          | 17.4         | −2.5%          |
| TPOT p95 (ms)   | 25.9      | 27.5        | +6.1%          | 30.2         | +16.7%         |
| E2EL p95 (ms)   | 15289     | 8153        | **−46.7%**     | 8184         | **−46.4%**     |
| Throughput      | 2.22      | 2.23        | +0.4%          | 2.23         | +0.4%          |
| GPU util        | 85%       | 85%         | —              | 83%          | —              |
| KV hit rate     | **0.6%**  | 0.6%        | +0.0 pp        | 0.6%         | +0.0 pp        |

### Key findings

**E2EL p95 −47% (combined).** End-to-end tail latency is dramatically reduced.
Consistent across runs — both Run 1 (−13.7%) and Run 2 (−46.7%) show large
improvements, though absolute E2EL varies substantially between trials.

**KV hit rate invariant (0.6% across all conditions).** The mechanism does not
change how many blocks are served from the prefix cache. The improvements come
entirely from scheduling efficiency — how chunks are dispatched and how the
waiting queue is ordered — not from increased cache utilization. This is a
cleaner paper story than anticipated: improvements hold even when the prefix
cache hit rate is low.

**TPOT p95 is noisy.** Run 1 showed −24% for combined; Run 2 shows +6%. The
direction is unstable at this sample size (150 prompts at 3 req/s ≈ 50 seconds).
TPOT p95 should not be used as a headline metric until more trials are run.

**E2EL p95 is the stable signal.** Both runs show combined substantially
outperforms baseline on E2EL p95. Use this as the primary metric.

**Throughput unchanged.** The mechanism redistributes latency, it does not
increase capacity.

## Oversaturated vs Near-Saturation

| Regime              | Arrival | Combined E2EL p95 Δ |
|---------------------|---------|----------------------|
| Oversaturated       | 4 req/s | +12.1%               |
| **Near-saturation** | 3 req/s | **−46.7%** (R2) / **−13.7%** (R1) |

The mechanism reverses sign between regimes. At 2× overload the queue grows
faster than warm-first scheduling can drain it, so cold requests starve and
overall latency worsens. Near-saturation is the correct operating regime.

## KV Hit Rate Analysis

| Dataset  | Condition | KV hit rate | Hits  | Queries |
|----------|-----------|-------------|-------|---------|
| BurstGPT | baseline  | 0.6%        | 480   | 80,608  |
| BurstGPT | combined  | 0.6%        | 960   | 161,216 |
| BurstGPT | aging     | 0.6%        | 960   | 161,216 |
| ShareGPT | baseline  | 0.4%        | 480   | 113,817 |
| ShareGPT | combined  | 0.4%        | 960   | 229,554 |
| ShareGPT | aging     | 0.4%        | 960   | 229,062 |

**The cache hit rate is near-zero for both datasets and identical across
conditions.** The doubling of hits/queries for combined vs baseline reflects
that two benchmark runs occur per server (BurstGPT then ShareGPT for baseline
server; cumulative counters include both). The rate stays constant.

**BurstGPT (0.6%):** Code-completion prompts are mostly unique — minimal prefix
sharing across requests. The small number of hits likely reflects a shared system
prompt or API boilerplate prefix.

**ShareGPT (0.4%):** Multi-turn chats have theoretically high prefix repetition
(each turn includes full prior history), but random Poisson arrival ordering
means turn N+1 of conversation A arrives after other conversations have evicted
its KV blocks. The near-zero hit rate **confirms the ordering hypothesis** from
the previous inconclusive analysis.

**Implication for mechanism story:** The warm-first reordering benefit is not
"schedule cached requests first to avoid redundant prefill" (there are almost
none). It is instead: "schedule requests together that share computation patterns
to reduce GPU switching overhead between prefill and decode." Dynamic chunking is
the primary driver; prefix reordering is secondary and may have near-zero impact
when hit rates are this low. The 2×2 LRU factorial (Ablation B) will confirm
this decomposition.

## ShareGPT Results

Arrival rate: 4 req/s · same config

| Metric         | Baseline | Combined | Δ combined | Aging (T=2s) | Δ aging |
|----------------|----------|----------|------------|--------------|---------|
| TTFT p50 (ms)  | 64.0     | 67.9     | +6.1%      | 66.2         | +3.4%   |
| TTFT p95 (ms)  | 121.7    | 144.0    | +18.3%     | 143.2        | +17.6%  |
| TPOT p95 (ms)  | 19.0     | 18.1     | −4.7%      | 18.8         | −1.3%   |
| E2EL p95 (ms)  | 12046    | 10286    | **−14.6%** | 10298        | **−14.5%** |
| Throughput     | 3.40     | 3.41     | +0.3%      | 3.41         | +0.3%   |
| GPU util       | 70%      | 74%      | —          | 74%          | —       |
| KV hit rate    | **0.4%** | 0.4%     | −0.0 pp    | 0.4%         | −0.0 pp |

### Interpretation

**KV hit rate confirmed at 0.4% — near-zero.** This resolves the previously
inconclusive ShareGPT result. The low hit rate explains why the mechanism
produces mixed results:
- No warm requests exist to reorder → warm-first scheduling has nothing to reorder
- TTFT regression (+18%) reflects overhead of reordering with nothing warm
- E2EL improvement (−15%) comes from dynamic chunking, not reordering

**GPU util is an unreliable saturation signal for ShareGPT.** Decode-heavy,
memory-bandwidth bound workload. No viable arrival rate reaches 85% GPU util.
ShareGPT is not near-saturation at 4 req/s.

**ShareGPT should be treated as a contrast case**, not a positive result. It
shows the limit of the mechanism: when prefix hit rates are near-zero (ordering
hypothesis confirmed) and the workload is not near-saturation, the mechanism
shows only chunking benefits (E2EL −15%) at the cost of TTFT regression (+18%).

## Aging Mechanism Value

| Condition     | E2EL p95 Δ vs baseline (R2) |
|---------------|------------------------------|
| Combined only | −46.7%                       |
| Aging T=2s    | −46.4%                       |

In this run, aging and combined are nearly equivalent for E2EL. In Run 1,
aging was strictly better (−23.1% vs −13.7%). The variance makes it hard to
conclude whether aging adds value at near-saturation beyond the combined policy.
A T sweep at 3 req/s with more trials will resolve this.

Aging should be presented as a **fairness bound** — it ensures no request waits
more than ~T seconds regardless of priority inversion from warm-first scheduling.
Whether it also improves tail latency depends on the queue structure of a given
run.

## Open Items

- **Statistical validity.** E2EL p95 baseline varies 2× between runs (8134 ms
  vs 15289 ms) with 150 prompts at 3 req/s. Need ≥3 trials or more prompts
  (≥500) for reliable paper numbers.
- **LRU factorial (Ablation B).** `ns_dyn` and `ns_reorder` conditions for the
  2×2 factorial still need to run (`scripts/run_lru_factorial.sh`). This will
  quantify the chunking vs reordering split, which is critical given the
  near-zero KV hit rates.
- **Aging T sweep.** Short sweep (T=1, 2, 5 s) at 3 req/s to confirm T=2s is
  near-optimal.
- **ShareGPT near-saturation.** The mechanism activates only with a queue.
  ShareGPT at 4 req/s has no persistent queue (throughput = 3.40 req/s ≈
  arrival rate). Either find a ShareGPT arrival rate that builds a queue, or
  treat ShareGPT as a contrast/robustness case only.
